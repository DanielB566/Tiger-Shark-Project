Tiger Sharks

Keystone Species Conservation Research Project

By Adam Nisenson and Daniel Bootnik

What is a Keystone species?


Species in an ecosystem that play a central role in the health of that ecosystem, and whose removal may cause the decline of the ecosystem. Tiger sharks are one of the many keystone speices

What is trophic cascade?


An ecological process triggered by the addition or removal of top predators, and the effect it has on the realtive population of other species in the food chain.

Tiger Shark facts.


- They can be found in tropical and subtropical waters around the world.

- They are opportunistic predators with a diverse diet that includes fish, seals, sea birds, squid, and even other sharks.

- They are one of the largest shark species, with individuals commonly reaching lengths of 10 to 14 feet (3 to 4 meters). Some even exceed that length.

Tiger Sharks ecosystem, their role, and threats.


Ecosystems: The tiger shark inhabits a variety of marine ecosystems, including coastal waters, coral reefs, and open ocean. They are found worldwide in warm and tropical seas.

Role: As apex predators Tiger Sharks help regulate the population of many species.

Threats: Due to overfishing Tiger Shark population has rapidly declined. Tiger Sharks also are at risk of finning which can leave the shark immobile for the rest of its live.

What would happen if Tiger Sharks declined, and how it would impact humans.


Climate Change: Tiger Sharks indirectly help control climate change. Without Tiger sharks their will be more Manatees to eat the seagrass.

Seagrass: CO2 is stored in seagrass so if there is less seagrass then CO2 emmisons will go up speeding up climate change (postive feedback loop).

Impact on humans: Climate change can lead to air pollution, extreme weather events, and increased hunger and poor nutrition.

How are people protecting tiger sharks? How can we help them?


WWF: They work to regulate finning, overfishing, and over consumption of sharks. They also help countries implement acts to protect sharks.

What can we do: I belive that that if we promote responsible fisherie management practices and put trade regulations in place to protect vulnerable shark species we can protect them.

Current Event: In west Australia tiger sharks declined by 71%. This caused one of Australia's worst heatwaves killing more then 90% of the seagrass in that area. This proves how Tiger Shark's help regulate climate change.
